Reviewer C
==============================

The main problem I have with the current presentation of the paper is that the
authors promise more than the paper contains. They argue at various places about
existing approaches to functional logic programming and why their approach is
simpler than other approaches. At this point I have to say that they are cheating
a bit: by ignoring the objectives of existing approaches to functional logic
programming, they present another approach which does not achieve these objectives
and argue that their approach is simpler and more elegant. Of course, if one drops
some objectives, one can present simpler solutions. Let me elaborate a bit on
this.

Logic programming is based on the idea to think and specify problems in an
abstract high-level manner (e.g., predicate logic) so that the detailed
computation steps to find a solution to a problem can be ignored. Soundness and
completeness of the operational semantics is an important objective to support
this idea. Since a truly functional logic language should support the same
properties (and, due to functions, in a more elegant and efficient manner), most
of the existing literature try to develop calculi ensuring these properties. In
particular, it has been shown that lazy evaluation is a key to achieve this goal:
without lazy evaluation, there is no advantage to add functions to logic programs
[1], and with a needed evaluation strategy [2], one can achieve completeness as
well as optimal evaluation. Since the combination of non-determinism and laziness
causes non-trivial problems (call-time vs. run-time choice, strong/rigid/weak
encapsulation,...), the existing approaches are sometimes complex.

In this sense, the current paper just gives up completeness and presents a new
calculus which looks simpler (e.g., elegant inclusion of encapsulation) but has
the problem that computations might get stuck, i.e., no result is computed (a
behavior avoided by an operationally complete strategy). It should be noted that
this approach to functional logic programming is not new, e.g., it is the basis of
the languages Goffin [3] or Escher [4] (where the computational model of Escher is
also based on applying simple rewrite steps). On the positive side, the current
paper provides a new calculus covering also encapsulation of choices (a
notoriously difficult problem in logic programming) and presents a (restricted)
confluence result. By being not lazy, optimality and completeness is not
supported, but this could be a matter of taste.

Another matter of taste is the kind of desired semantics. The authors argue
several times that their small-step calculus is more easy for programmers,
compared to big-step or heap-based semantics. However, their semantics could have
the same problem. Since the heap contents is encoded in VC as a collection of
equations (aka let bindings), the expressions to be manipulated become
big. Actually, it was not easy for me to perform some example evaluations in VC in
order to understand its behavior on concrete examples. BTW, this is the motivation
for more abstract views, e.g., equational logic reasoning, of functional logic
programming.

Due to the matter of taste of these views, I would not argue against this
paper. Actually, I think it is an interesting contribution to extend programming
paradigms and might initiate discussions at ICFP. However, I strongly encourage
the authors to calm down and be honest in their statements. As I discussed above,
there are different approaches to functional logic programming, like
narrowing-based where completeness is important (SLOG, K-Leaf, Babel, Toy,
Curry,...) and residuation-based which give up completeness for a more
deterministic behavior (Goffin, Escher,...) where computations might get
stuck. This approach falls into the latter category (but has new aspects compared
to the existing approaches). For instance, it is not clear how an operationally
complete strategy, where non-determinism or fair scheduling is important [5], can
be compiled into the deterministic VC, as it is mentioned in the paper. Thus, it
is a calculus for a specific class of functionla logic languages. If the paper is
accepted, the title should be changed to reflect the limits of this
calculus. Since the calculus does not support completeness as other calculi but
emphasize confluence and deterministic order of results (if results are obtained),
a title like "...a Calculus for Deterministic/Confluent Functional Logic
Programming" would be more adequate.

Altogether, the paper is very-well written and is an interesting contribution to functional logic programming. If accepted, it should be better related to existing works (see also my comments below) and the title must be adapted in this sense.

Reviewer D
==============================
I welcome that the paper first introduces the calculus informally by discussing
various example programs instead of directly introducing the rewrite
semantics. This helped me greatly to get a feel for the calculus before diving
into the details.

It seems to me that the authors have a clear target use-case in mind which guide
some design decisions of the calculus. For example: why does the calculus have to
be deterministic, or why not define built-in booleans and instead design
conditionals based on the existence of a value? Without understanding the target
use-case, the design decisions seem somewhat arbitrary and it is hard to follow
and assess them.

In the same vein, the paper does not provide any justification for the need of
this calculus. In particular, the paper does not present any significant case
studies highlighting the strengths of VC. I believe this paper can be stronger if
the authors highlight specific problems that can be elegantly solved when using
the Verse language justifying the need for the language, and therefore the
calculus.

The paper does not discuss a line of related work that combines Datalog with
functional programming. How does this calculus compare to approaches such as
Datafun [1], Flix [2], Formulog [3], and functional IncA [4]?

Subsection 3.4 feels out of place. I don't understand why the program with x = 100
gets stuck. The reader needs more guidance to understand what the exact problem
is. Maybe then it becomes clear what the point of the subsection is, but I don't
know what this section is trying to convey.
